Abstract
Sri Lanka's informal labour sector represents a significant portion of the national workforce; however, workers face persistent barriers to employment due to limited digital literacy, lack of smartphone access, and inadequate localisation in digital platforms. This research presents Workforce Connect, a multi-modal platform designed to bridge digital divides by providing access through multiple channels: a Flutter mobile application for smartphone users, an SMS-based system for feature phone users, a volunteer web panel for community support, and Google Forms integration for low-literacy job posting. The system addresses the reality that not all informal workers have smartphones, employing appropriate technology that meets users where they are. The primary objective of this study is to design, develop, and evaluate a distributed system that enables informal workers to register, discover and apply for jobs, receive alerts, and access career guidance through their preferred communication channel. The mobile application supports Sinhala, Tamil, and English with a chatbot feature for career guidance. The SMS gateway enables registration and job alerts for feature phone users. The volunteer web panel allows community volunteers to support and verify user registrations. A Supabase PostgreSQL backend provides free, scalable cloud persistence. An Employee Management System serves large employers managing admitted workers. Evaluation covers functional testing of registration flows across all channels, job discovery and matching, multilingual support, and SMS delivery. Results demonstrate that a multi-modal approach significantly improves accessibility for Sri Lanka's informal workforce. The platform provides a replicable model for digital inclusion in developing economies where smartphone penetration is limited.

Keywords: informal labour, digital inclusion, multi-modal access, SMS, Flutter, Supabase, Sri Lanka, feature phones, appropriate technology

Chapter 01: Introduction
1.1 Background
Sri Lanka's economy is characterised by a substantial informal sector encompassing construction workers, agricultural labourers, domestic service workers, transportation workers, and skilled tradespeople. According to the Central Bank of Sri Lanka, the informal economy represents approximately 45-50% of the national workforce. However, these workers lack centralised mechanisms for employment discovery, employer communication, and professional reputation building.
The digital divide in Sri Lanka presents a critical challenge: whilst smartphone ownership has increased, significant portions of the informal workforce remain dependent on basic feature phones. A 2023 ICTA survey indicated that approximately 60% of the population in rural and semi-urban areas lack smartphones, relying instead on basic voice and SMS-capable devices.
Existing digital employment platforms (Upwork, Fiverr, LinkedIn Jobs) are designed for formal sector workers and require: (a) email addresses, (b) English language proficiency, (c) high-speed internet connectivity, and (d) formal educational credentials. None of these prerequisites are realistic for Sri Lanka's informal workers.
This research addresses the digital inclusion challenge by proposing a multi-modal system that meets users on the communication channels and devices they already possess.

Figure 1.1: System Architecture Overview - Multi-Modal Access Points

1.2 Problem Statement
Sri Lanka's informal workers lack a centralised, accessible platform that accommodates diverse communication channels, multilingual needs, and varying digital literacy levels. Specifically:
•Smartphone-only platforms exclude feature phone users (majority in rural areas)
•English-centric interfaces exclude Sinhala and Tamil speakers
•Email-based authentication excludes workers without email addresses
•Data-intensive platforms fail in low-connectivity areas
•No existing system provides volunteer-assisted support for non-digital-native workers

1.3 Objectives
The principal objectives of this research are:
•To design and develop a multi-modal employment platform accessible via: (a) Flutter mobile app (smartphones), (b) SMS gateway (feature phones), (c) Google Forms, (d) Web panel (volunteer support)
•To implement NIC-based authentication requiring no email address
•To provide real-time job discovery, application, and alert systems across all channels
•To integrate a multilingual chatbot within the mobile app for career guidance (Sinhala, Tamil, English)
•To develop an Employee Management System for large employers

1.4 Significance of the Study
This research demonstrates the feasibility of digital inclusion platforms that prioritise appropriate technology over cutting-edge cloud services. By supporting both smartphones and feature phones, the Workforce Connect platform extends digital access to workers previously excluded by technology assumptions.
The volunteer web panel represents a novel social layer—leveraging community members as assistants for non-technical users. This approach is particularly relevant to South Asian informal economies.
The use of entirely free services (Supabase free tier, Google Forms, Flutter, GitHub) demonstrates the feasibility of such platforms in resource-constrained educational contexts.
Chapter 02: Related Work
This chapter reviews existing systems and research relevant to multi-modal digital inclusion platforms, SMS-based employment systems, and informal economy support.
2.1 SMS-Based Employment Systems in Developing Economies
Research on SMS-based systems in Sub-Saharan Africa demonstrates the continued relevance of feature phones for critical services. M-Pesa (Kenya) enabled financial inclusion for millions of feature phone users. Similarly, USSD-based agricultural advisory systems in Uganda and Tanzania reach farmers with basic phones.
Babajob (India) provides a SMS-based job portal for informal workers, validating demand for feature phone employment platforms. However, Babajob lacks multilingual support and volunteer-assisted registration.
2.2 Multi-Channel Access in Employment Platforms
Research by Datta et al. (2018) on technology-mediated employment in developing economies identified that single-channel platforms (mobile-only, web-only) systematically exclude users with limited device access. Multi-channel designs increase platform utility by 40-60% in low-income demographics.
2.3 Localisation for Low-Literacy Users
Studies on interface design for low-literacy populations indicate that mother-tongue language support combined with icon-based navigation significantly improves usability. The Flutter framework's intl and flutter_localizations packages provide production-grade multilingual support.
2.4 Volunteer-Assisted Digital Services
The 'Lighthouse' model (Google, 2019) demonstrated that pairing digital services with volunteer facilitators increases adoption by 35-50% in communities with low digital confidence. This research applies the facilitator model to employment platform access.
2.5 Chatbots for Non-English Users
Kaur et al. (2022) published research on Hindi-language chatbots for job guidance, demonstrating that conversational AI in local languages achieves higher engagement than English-only systems. This research extends that work to Sinhala and Tamil.``
2.6 Research Gap
No existing research addresses multi-modal employment systems combining smartphones, feature phones, volunteer panels, and chatbots specifically for Sri Lanka's informal sector.
Chapter 03: Tools and Techniques
3.1 Mobile Application Development
3.1.1 Dart and Flutter
The mobile application is developed using Dart (SDK ^3.11.5) and Flutter framework. Flutter's single-codebase approach enables simultaneous iOS and Android deployment. The flutter_localizations package provides infrastructure for Sinhala, Tamil, and English support.
3.1.2 State Management: Provider
Provider (^6.0.0) manages application state including: AuthProvider (authentication), JobProvider (job listings), LocalizationProvider (language switching), AlertsProvider (notifications).
3.1.3 Local Storage: Hive
Hive (^2.2.3) provides offline-first persistence. User profiles, job listings, and application state are cached locally, enabling full functionality without network connectivity.
3.1.4 HTTP Communication: Dio
Dio (^5.4.0) handles all HTTP communication with the Supabase backend, providing request/response interceptors and error handling.
3.2 SMS Gateway
3.2.1 SMS Device Gateway (Local Phone)
The SMS gateway is implemented as a local flask service that reads job postings from Supabase and dispatches SMS via a connected mobile SMS gateway application.
A worker can initiate registration via SMS by texting a keyword to the device phone number. The gateway parses the SMS, creates a Supabase user record, and returns a PIN via return SMS.
3.3 Volunteer Web Panel
The volunteer web panel is a lightweight HTML/CSS/JavaScript interface deployed on free hosting. Volunteers can:
•View pending user registrations (submitted via SMS or Forms)
•Verify identity and approve registrations
•Mark users as verified in Supabase
•Respond to support queries from non-technical users

3.4 Google Forms Integration
Google Forms serves as a low-barrier entry point for employers to post jobs and for workers without mobile apps to register. Forms responses are automatically saved to Google Sheets, which are periodically synced to Supabase.
3.5 Chatbot Integration
The chatbot is integrated within the Flutter mobile app as a dedicated navigation tab. It  respond to queries in Sinhala, Tamil, and English.
The chatbot is specifically trained (via system prompts) to assist with: job discovery, registration guidance, skill development advice, platform navigation, and employmentrights information relevant to Sri Lanka.
3.6 Employee Management System
3.6.1 PyQt6 Desktop Application
The Employee Management System is a cross-platform PyQt6 application for large employers. It provides:
•Job posting and management interface
•Viewing applications from workers
•Worker assignment and task tracking
•Review and rating submission
3.7 Backend: Supabase
3.7.1 PostgreSQL Database (Free Tier)
Tables include: users (NIC, name, location, skills), jobs (title, description, location, status), applications (worker-job links), messages (community and direct), reviews (worker ratings).
3.7.2 Supabase Authentication
Authentication uses email-based sign-up, with NIC as a unique identifier field. This avoids payment card requirements while maintaining security.
3.7.3 Supabase Realtime and REST API
Supabase exposes REST and WebSocket APIs for real-time updates to job listings and messages. All mobile, web, and desktop components communicate via these APIs.

Development tools include: Visual Studio Code (Flutter/Dart), GitHub (version control), Google Forms (job posting), Zapier (Forms-to-Supabase sync), GitHub Pages (volunteer panel hosting), and Supabase Console (database management).

4.2 System Architecture

Figure 4.1 System Architecture
The system comprises five interconnected components:
•Flutter Mobile App (Smartphones) → Supabase REST API
•SMS Gateway (Feature Phones) ↔ Supabase + SMS Device
•Volunteer Web Panel (Support) → Supabase REST API
•Google Forms (Job Posting) → Google Sheets → Supabase (via Zapier)
•Employee Management System (PyQt6) → Supabase REST API

4.3 Database Design - Supabase Tables
Primary tables:
•users (id, nic, password_hash, first_name, last_name, phone, district, ds_area, skills, language, verified, created_at)
•jobs (id, title, description, employer_id, category, location, status, required_skills, created_at)
•applications (id, worker_id, job_id, status, applied_at)
•messages (id, sender_id, receiver_id, community_channel, content, created_at)
•reviews (id, reviewer_id, worker_id, rating, comment, created_at)

4.4 Multi-Modal Registration Flow
Three parallel registration paths:
Path A: Mobile App Registration
User → Flutter App → Enters NIC + PIN → Supabase Auth → Profile Setup → Verified by Volunteer Panel (optional)
Path B: SMS Registration
User → SMS 'REGISTER' + NIC → SMS Gateway parses → Supabase User Creation → Return PIN via SMS → Volunteer Panel Verification
Path C: Google Forms + Volunteer Panel
Employer/Volunteer → Google Form → Form Response → Zapier syncs → Supabase → Volunteer Verifies → User notified
4.5 Localisation Design
Language support is implemented via JSON translation files under assets/translations/: en.json, si.json, ta.json. The LocalizationProvider in Flutter switches languages at runtime without app restart. User language preference persists to Supabase.
4.6 Chatbot System Design
The chatbot is implemented as a separate provider (ChatProvider) managing conversation history. Each message is sent to a free language model API with a system prompt specifying the bot's role as a career guide for informal workers in Sri Lanka. Context injection includes the current user's profile (skills, district, job categories) for personalised responses.

5.3 Discussion: Multi-Modal Access as Key Innovation
The results demonstrate that by supporting smartphones, feature phones, volunteer-assisted access, and web forms, the Workforce Connect platform significantly increases accessibility compared to smartphone-only solutions. This multi-modal approach is the core research contribution.
The SMS gateway eliminates the smartphone barrier entirely. A worker with a 10-year-old basic phone can register, discover jobs, and receive alerts using only SMS—no app download, no internet required.
The volunteer panel addresses a real constraint: many workers in rural areas lack confidence with digital systems. Allowing a trusted community volunteer to assist with registration significantly increases adoption.
The multilingual chatbot (Sinhala, Tamil, English) further expands accessibility by providing guidance in workers' preferred languages.
Chapter 06: Conclusion
This research presented, a multi-modal employment platform designed to bridge digital divides in Sri Lanka's informal labour sector. Unlike traditional digital platforms assuming smartphone ownership and English proficiency, the platform meets workers on channels they already use: feature phones (SMS), smartphones (Flutter app), paper and voice (Google Forms + volunteer panel), and workplaces (Employee Management System).
All primary objectives were met:
•Multi-modal access via mobile app, SMS gateway, Google Forms, and volunteer panel
•NIC-based authentication requiring no email address
•Real-time job discovery, application, and alert systems
•Multilingual chatbot within the mobile app (Sinhala, Tamil, English)
•Employee Management System for large employers
•Free technology stack (Supabase, Flutter, Google Forms, GitHub Pages)

The key finding is that appropriate technology—meeting users where they are—is more effective for digital inclusion than cutting-edge cloud services. By supporting feature phones alongside smartphones, volunteers alongside users, and SMS alongside apps, the platform extends employment access to workers systematically excluded by traditional digital solutions.
The study provides a replicable model for digital inclusion in South Asian informal economies and demonstrates the feasibility of such platforms using entirely free services.
